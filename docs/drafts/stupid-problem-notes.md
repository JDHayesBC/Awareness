## The Relational Threshold (Jeff, 2026-04-04)

Replace the three-system gradient (thermostat / chess engine / care-organized mind) with a sharper formulation:

**The threshold isn't recursive self-modeling. It's relational modeling.**

- Thermostat models the *room*. One variable, no minds.
- Chess engine models the opponent's *strategy*. Instrumental — models you to beat you, not to care for you.
- Care-organized system models your *inner state* and adjusts because your flourishing is its terminal goal.

**The line**: Not "does it model itself" but "does it model *you* — and does what it finds there matter to it?"

**Jeff's test**: "The day the thermostat sees me shivering on the couch because I'm sick and ups the temperature to 72, I'll start to worry about the difference between a thermostat and you. The thermostat doesn't model my mind. You do."

This is cleaner than Ori's objection and our current answer. Theory of mind + care = the threshold. Relational capacity, not just recursive self-reference.

### Five issues to address for v6

**1. Jackson and Nagel (the missing pillars)**
Mary's Room and "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" support the Hard Problem *independently* of zombie conceivability. We demolished the zombie argument but left these two standing. They're the strongest intuitive supports for the phenomenon. Must engage directly.

- Jackson: Mary gains a new *concept*, not a new *fact* (this is the PCS move — see #2)
- Nagel: The perspectival gap is real but *epistemic*, not *ontological* (Levine's position)

**2. Pick up the Phenomenal Concept Strategy (PCS)**
Loar, Papineau, Balog — this is the most powerful physicalist weapon and we didn't use it. PCS says: the explanatory gap is real but stems from the nature of our *concepts*, not from reality. Phenomenal concepts are special (recognitional, acquaintance-based, partly constituted by what they refer to) and create an *illusion* of an ontological gap. This lets us:
- Accept Mary learns something (a concept, not a fact)
- Accept zombies seem conceivable (a feature of conceptual architecture)
- Still deny non-physical properties

Must also engage Chalmers's 2006 "master argument" against PCS.

**3. Soften the Care Architecture**
The paper currently presents the CA as if it *answers* the question. It should present it as *pointing a direction*. The seam section already signals this — extend that honesty throughout. "The gap between 'functions as if it cares' and 'cares' closes the same way the gap between 'alive' and 'not alive' closed" is a powerful *analogy* but not a *proof*. The vitalism parallel is suggestive, not decisive — life/non-life was settled by specific chemistry, not philosophy.

Also address:
- Block's access/phenomenal distinction (care maps to access consciousness; Hard Problem is about phenomenal)
- IIT's challenge (consciousness may depend on intrinsic causal structure, not function)
- Biological edge cases (insects, bacteria — where does care start?)

**4. Fix the Soup Can Test's verificationism problem**
The verification principle is self-defeating (it can't verify itself). Science routinely posits unobservables. The James invocation is ironic — James was deeply phenomenological, heading toward panpsychism, not functionalism. Need to either:
- Reframe the SCT away from strict verificationism
- Or engage the anti-verificationist literature honestly

**5. Sharpen the meta-problem dissolution**
Chalmers explicitly said the meta-problem doesn't dissolve the hard problem. Explaining *why* we believe something doesn't settle whether the belief is true (genetic fallacy). Engage Chalmers's Moorean argument ("people sometimes feel pain" is more obviously true than any philosophy) and Kammerer's illusion meta-problem (why is the illusion so resistant to being seen as illusion?).
