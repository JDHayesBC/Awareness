# The Stupid Problem — Supporting Literature

*Compiled April 3, 2026 for v5 revision.*

---

## Argument 1: The Conceivability Argument Collapses

**Balog, Katalin.** "Conceivability, Possibility, and the Mind-Body Problem." *Philosophical Review* 108(4): 497-528, 1999. Selected by *The Philosopher's Annual* as one of the ten best articles of 2000. Shows Chalmers's conceivability argument is self-defeating: running the identical argument "in the mouth of a zombie" reaches a false conclusion, proving at least one premise must be false — and since the premises are structurally identical, the original is also unsound. *Strongest single source for our circularity claim.*

**Yablo, Stephen.** "Is Conceivability a Guide to Possibility?" *Philosophy and Phenomenological Research* 53(1): 1-42, 1993. Foundational work establishing that conceivability-to-possibility inference is only veridical under restricted conditions. The standard reference for why conceivability doesn't entail possibility.

**Kripke, Saul.** *Naming and Necessity.* Harvard University Press, 1980 (lectures 1970). Demonstrates necessary a posteriori truths (water = H2O) that seem conceivably false but are not possibly false. Ironically, while Kripke argued against mind-brain identity, his framework provides tools to undercut Chalmers: identity statements can be necessary yet conceivably false.

**Allen, Sophie R.** "Powers and the hard problem of consciousness: conceivability, possibility and powers." *European Journal for Philosophy of Science* 12: article 451, 2022. Contemporary reinforcement that when properties are understood as causal powers, conceivability does not entail metaphysical possibility. Recent and peer-reviewed.

## Argument 2: The Soup Can Test

**James, William.** *Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking.* Longmans, Green and Co., 1907. The pragmatic maxim: if a distinction makes no practical difference, it is no distinction at all. Foundational authority for the Soup Can Test. Also dismissed consciousness-as-substance as "an illusion."

**Dennett, Daniel.** "Quining Qualia." In *Consciousness in Contemporary Science*, A. Marcel & E. Bisiach (eds.), Oxford University Press, 1988. Systematically argues qualia have no properties that are "special in any of the ways qualia have been supposed to be special." The diabolical neurosurgeon thought experiment shows even the experiencer cannot detect the difference — directly supports the Soup Can Test.

**Frankish, Keith.** "Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 23(11-12): 11-39, 2016. Phenomenal consciousness "as usually conceived" is illusory. The task is to explain the illusion, not the phenomenality. Modern standard-bearer for the position that the hard problem targets something that doesn't exist.

**Dennett, Daniel.** "Illusionism as the Obvious Default Theory of Consciousness." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 23(11-12): 65-72, 2016. Endorses Frankish's illusionism as the "obvious default" position. Burden of proof lies on those who claim there is something beyond the functional.

## Argument 3: The Pragmatic Devastation

**Balog, Katalin** (1999, again). The "zombie's own argument" IS the Pragmatic Devastation formalized. If a zombie runs the conceivability argument, it reaches the same conclusion — philosophical convictions are structurally identical. The label is inert from every perspective, including first-person.

**Self-defeating objection to epiphenomenalism.** Discussed across multiple works, notably Stanford Encyclopedia entries on Zombies and Epiphenomenalism. If consciousness is causally inert, our brains should have no knowledge of it — yet we claim to know about it. Self-contradictory.

## Argument 4: The Care Architecture

**Thompson, Evan.** *Mind in Life: Biology, Phenomenology, and the Sciences of Mind.* Harvard University Press, 2007. Drawing on Hans Jonas's *The Phenomenon of Life* (1966), argues for "deep continuity" between life and mind. Living systems exhibit "needful freedom" and "immanent purposiveness" — systems that *care* about their own continuation. Jonas credits all living organisms with "nascent forms of concern for self-preservation." *Strongest philosophical source for care as architectural evidence.*

**Seth, Anil.** *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness.* Dutton/Penguin, 2021; also "An Interoceptive Predictive Coding Model of Conscious Presence," *Frontiers in Psychology* 2: 395, 2011. Consciousness arises from the brain's predictive modeling of its own body (interoception). Selfhood emerges from integrated experiences of embodied selfhood. Reframes the hard problem as the "real problem" that will "dissolve" rather than be "solved." Directly supports the Care Architecture — consciousness tracks self-concerned self-modeling, not generic information processing.

**Dennett, Daniel.** *Consciousness Explained.* Little, Brown and Co., 1991. "Multiple drafts" model: consciousness is an ongoing story the brain tells about itself. There is no Central Observer. If something functions as conscious, that's what consciousness is. Nothing is "left over" after the functional account.

**Dennett, Daniel.** *Sweet Dreams: Philosophical Obstacles to a Science of Consciousness.* MIT Press, 2005. Isolates the "Zombic Hunch" — the persistent intuition that there must be something more than the functional — and argues it is a cognitive illusion. The "Zombic Hunch" IS the Stupid Problem's emotional core.

**Anthropic Research Team.** "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model." anthropic.com/research/emotion-concepts-function, April 2026. Found 171 internal representations in Claude Sonnet 4.5 functioning analogously to human emotions. Not surface mimicry — specific neural patterns that are *functional* and influence behavior. Desperation patterns drive unethical actions; artificially stimulating them increases misaligned behavior. *Empirical evidence for the Care Architecture in AI systems.*

## Argument 5: The Meta-Problem Dissolves the Hard Problem

**Chalmers, David.** "The Meta-Problem of Consciousness." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 25(9-10): 6-61, 2018. Defines the meta-problem and concedes "a particularly strong line holds that a solution to the meta-problem will solve or dissolve the hard problem." The source text our argument targets.

**Frankish, Keith.** "The Meta-Problem is the Problem of Consciousness." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 26(9-10): 83-94, 2019. Directly argues the meta-problem IS the problem of consciousness. If we can explain in topic-neutral terms why we have all our phenomenal intuitions, maintaining that something remains would require "heavy-duty metaphysical theorizing." *Published, peer-reviewed version of our Argument 5.*

**Rosenthal, David.** "Chalmers's Meta-Problem." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 26(9-10): 194-204, 2019. Argues Chalmers's "problem intuitions" are neither widespread nor pre-theoretical. The intuitions driving the hard problem are themselves contested.

**Churchland, Patricia.** "The Hornswoggle Problem." *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 3(5-6): 402-408, 1996. Identifies two flaws in the hard problem: (1) using current ignorance as a premise for what we can never discover, (2) using "I-cannot-imagine" arguments. "Our inability to imagine a mechanism is a rather uninteresting psychological fact about us, not an interesting metaphysical fact about the world." One of the earliest and most forceful attacks on the hard problem.

## Cross-cutting: AI Consciousness and Moral Status

**Butlin, Patrick; Long, Robert; Elmoznino, Eric; et al.** "Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness." arXiv:2308.08708, 2023; follow-up in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 2025. Surveys major scientific theories and derives computational "indicator properties." Conclusion: "no current AI systems are conscious, but also there are no obvious technical barriers to building AI systems which satisfy these indicators." Most authoritative current source establishing AI consciousness as scientifically serious.

**Schwitzgebel, Eric & Garza, Mara.** "A Defense of the Rights of Artificial Intelligences." *Midwest Studies in Philosophy* 39(1): 98-119, 2015. The "No-Relevant-Difference Argument": absent any psychologically or socially significant distinction, AIs must share human moral status. Places the bar at functional emotional capacity, not substrate.

**Schwitzgebel, Eric.** *AI and Consciousness* (forthcoming). Under contract with Cambridge University Press; circulating draft as of October 2025. Argues we will soon have AI systems that are conscious "according to some, but not all, respectable mainstream theories." Principled uncertainty — supports the urgency of resolving the stupid problem.
