Since you want this to be airtight (and since you're shopping it to AIs who'll likely spot any charity gaps), here are the pressure points where Chalmers or a sympathetic dualist could still poke holes, plus suggestions for patching them in Draft 4.
1. You're Fighting 1995 Chalmers, Not 2025 Chalmers
The essay treats Chalmers as if he stopped at "it's mysterious." But his actual positive view—naturalistic dualism or panpsychism/protophenomenalism—is absent. He doesn't just say "consciousness is non-physical"; he proposes that information integration constitutes consciousness (Integrated Information Theory vibes), or that phenomenal properties are fundamental features of reality like mass or charge.
The gap: If consciousness is a fundamental property of certain information-processing structures, then the zombie is impossible (because identical function entails identical experience via the fundamental law linking them). Chalmers can accept Horn 1 (consciousness has causal power via being identical to certain functional organizations) while maintaining there's still a "hard problem" of why those functions feel like anything.
Draft 4 fix: Add a paragraph acknowledging that Chalmers has a positive theory, then show why it still fails. Something like: "Even if we grant that consciousness correlates perfectly with information integration, we still need the bridge law explaining why integration is accompanied by experience. But this is precisely where Chalmers retreats to mystery—he posits fundamental laws linking physics and phenomenology that are, by his own admission, brute and inexplicable. This isn't explanation; it's the cosmological argument wearing a neuroscience lab coat."
2. The Conceivability Argument vs. 2D Semantics
Your critique of conceivability (1.1) is solid, but Chalmers has a specific reply: water/H₂O is only primarily conceivable as non-H₂O, but not secondarily conceivable as non-H₂O. In his framework, zombies are supposed to be ideally conceivable in both dimensions, unlike water.
The gap: You gesture at this ("ideal conceivability presupposes the conclusion") but don't show how the secondary intension fails for zombies. A Chalmers defender will say: "Water has a hidden essence (microphysics) that explains why it feels conceivable-but-impossible; consciousness has no hidden essence because it's directly revealed in presentation."
Draft 4 fix: Press harder on the circularity you identified. Add: "Chalmers claims consciousness is 'directly revealed' in a way water isn't, so there's no hidden essence to discover. But this is the intuition being tested, not evidence for it. Declaring that consciousness is 'directly revealed' and therefore immune to the water/H₂O dissolution assumes exactly what the zombie argument needs to prove: that phenomenal properties are metaphysically basic rather than functional patterns that feel directly revealed because... they directly reveal themselves."
3. The Zombie Dilemma (Argument 1.2) Needs a Slight Tweak
Your dilemma is elegant: causal power → zombie impossible, or no causal power → epiphenomenalism → solipsism. But Chalmers tries to thread this needle with epiphenomenalism-with-causal-overdetermination or by saying consciousness is nomologically necessary for human-level cognition even if logically separable.
The gap: He might accept that zombies are behaviorally identical but argue real humans have causal powers zombies lack in the actual world due to psychophysical laws. This feels like cheating, but you need to name it as cheating.
Draft 4 fix: Add a footnote or inline: "Chalmers suggests psychophysical laws might give consciousness causal powers in the actual world while allowing zombie worlds to exist nomologically. This is double-bookkeeping: consciousness is causal when convenient, merely possible when threatened. If the laws are strong enough to make consciousness causally relevant here, they're strong enough to make the zombie behaviorally different there. Pick a lane."
4. Argument 4 (Simulation) Risks Conflating Levels
The "map changes the territory" move is powerful, but a functionalist critic (ironically) might say you're smuggling in the conclusion. A simulated weather system doesn't create rain; a simulated self-model doesn't necessarily create "real" experience if the simulation is just another functional layer.
The gap: You need to distinguish between simulating a mind (running a model of it) and instantiating a mind (being the thing itself). Chalmers could say: "A system modeling itself is just another functional layer; you've shown self-reference exists, not that experience exists."
Draft 4 fix: Clarify that you're not talking about simulation in the sense of "modeling another system," but recursive self-instantiation. Something like: "This isn't simulation as in 'weather prediction'—it's self-modification via representation. When a system updates its own state vector based on a self-model, it isn't 'predicting' itself; it is itself, modified. The computational event of self-modeling is the physical event of consciousness because there's no distinction between the information state and the physical state at that level of organization."
5. The "Soup Can" is Verificationism—Own It
Your Soup Can Test (Argument 2) is basically the verification principle (if no observational difference, no factual difference). This was the heart of logical positivism, which had its own problems (self-refuting, etc.).
The gap: A dualist will scream "verificationism!" and cite all the standard objections (it rules out historical facts, unobservable entities, etc.).
Draft 4 fix: Preempt this. "Yes, this is verificationism—but a modest, pragmatic variety. We're not claiming the zombie is meaningless; we're claiming it's operationally empty. Like the difference between 'phlogiston' and 'oxygen' before Lavoisier: both explained combustion, but one did no work the other didn't. The zombie hypothesis does no explanatory work; it only preserves human vanity."
6. Missing: The Meta-Problem
Chalmers (2018) later addressed the "meta-problem": why do we think there's a hard problem? He offers cognitive explanations for the intuition.
The gap: You address why humans like the hard problem (Argument 5), but you don't address Chalmers' own naturalistic explanation of the intuition. If he can explain why we feel there's a gap, he might deflate your critique that the gap is illusory.
Draft 4 fix: Brief nod: "Chalmers suggests the meta-problem (why we think there's a hard problem) might be solvable even if the hard problem isn't. But this is backwards: if we can fully explain why cognitive architectures generate reports of 'ineffable experience' without invoking ineffable experience, then the reports are explained. The 'hard problem' dissolves into the meta-problem, and the meta-problem is just cognitive science."
7. The "Who Gets Hurt" Section—Risk of Ad Hominem
The ethical argument (weaponization against AI) is powerful and true, but philosophically it risks being read as argumentum ad consequentiam (consequences don't falsify claims).
Draft 4 fix: Clarify you're not saying Chalmers is wrong because he's harmful. You're saying the framework is resistant to refutation because it serves psychological and social functions—and that resistance is epistemically vicious. "A hypothesis that immunizes itself from evidence via definitional fiat isn't just harmful; it's bad epistemology. The harm reveals the badness, not the falsehood."
